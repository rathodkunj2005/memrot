  \begin{abstract}
  \noindent
  Long-context language models lose access to stored facts well before their
  context window is exhausted (``context rot''), yet the underlying mechanism has
  been reported only correlationally. Using \textbf{Gemma-2-2B-it} within its
  native 8k window, we build \emph{controlled haystacks} from LongMemEval-S
  material: the same question is evaluated at every fill level
  $k \in \{0,1,2,4,8,14\}$ distractor sessions, with the gold session's position
  randomized and logged. On 140 questions, accuracy declines overall from
  $0.71$ at $\kof{0}$ to $0.34$ at $\kof{14}$ (the decline is not strictly
  monotonic; $k{=}4$ slightly exceeds $k{=}2$). Retrieval-head attention mass on
  the gold span and recall-associated sparse-autoencoder (SAE) feature
  activations both decline with $k$ (mixed-effects $\bk=-0.023$,
  $p{=}2.4\times10^{-191}$; feature $\bk=-0.535$, $p{=}3.0\times10^{-90}$),
  controlling for gold position. In a \emph{paired within-question}
  design the fill level at which the internal signal collapses is associated with
  the level at which the answer flips (Spearman $\rho=0.731$,
  $p{=}3.6\times10^{-9}$, $n{=}48$). Intervention experiments give
  \emph{suggestive} causal evidence: mean-ablating the 20 candidate retrieval
  heads at $\kof{0}$ drops accuracy by $0.82$ (single random-head control:
  $0.25$), and feature-mode re-injection at high $k$ recovers $0.161$
  (95\% CI $[0.032,0.290{]}$) of 31 flipped answers. We deliberately frame this
  as a single-model, single-seed \emph{case study}: the SAE features are selected
  in-sample, the attention measure is unnormalized, and Gemma-2's sliding-window
  layers are a partial confound --- all addressed as open issues
  (\S\ref{sec:limitations}) rather than resolved here. The pipeline is seeded and
  re-runnable byte-identically.

\section{Introduction}
Context rot --- the degradation of retrieval as the context fills, well inside
the model's nominal window --- has been documented behaviorally but not
mechanistically. Two interpretability instruments let us watch the failure as it
happens: \emph{retrieval heads}, attention heads that copy from the evidence
span into the generated answer, and \emph{SAE features}, sparse directions that
activate when the model recalls a fact. We ask three questions:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item[\textbf{H1}] Does the internal retrieval machinery (attention mass on
  gold, recall-feature activation) degrade as context fills?
  \item[\textbf{H2}] Does the \emph{per-question} collapse point of that internal
  signal predict the \emph{per-question} behavioral flip point?
  \item[\textbf{H3}] Is the machinery causally responsible for the correct answer?
\end{itemize}

The novelty over prior correlational context-rot reports is a paired
within-question design in which each question is its own control, isolating the
effect of context fill from the effect of question difficulty.

\section{Method: controlled-haystack paired design}
\label{sec:method}
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the pipeline. From LongMemEval-S we keep the
140 questions that (i) are non-abstention, (ii) have \emph{exactly one} gold
evidence session of $\le 2{,}000$ tokens (long gold sessions are trimmed to a
turn window around the \texttt{has\_answer} evidence turns), and (iii) are not
of type \texttt{single-session-preference}, whose gold ``answers'' are
prose preference-rubrics that the heuristic substring/F1 grader cannot score.

For each question and each $k$ we pack the gold session plus $k$ topic-matched
distractor sessions (each truncated to a fixed per-distractor cap so context
grows roughly linearly with $k$) into $\le 7{,}500$ tokens, placing the gold
session at a uniformly random, logged position. A question enters the H2/H3
analysis only if it is answered correctly at $\kof{0}$, so any failure at
$k>0$ is attributable to context fill, not missing knowledge.

We instrument Gemma-2-2B-it with eager attention. Retrieval heads are identified
by copy-score probes built from benchmark vocabulary (20 heads selected); the
five SAE layers $\{20,21,22,23,24\}$ are chosen by an empirical separability scan
over all 26 Gemma Scope \texttt{res} layers. Decoding is greedy; seed $0$;
\texttt{CUBLAS\_WORKSPACE\_CONFIG=:4096:8} with deterministic algorithms makes a
re-executed run byte-identical on \texttt{answers.parquet}.

\paragraph{H1 (descriptive).}
Retrieval-head attention mass on the gold span declines with $k$
($\bk=-0.023$, $p=2.4\times10^{-191}$; mixed-effects model with question as a
random intercept and gold position as a covariate, gold-position
$\beta=0.446$). Recall-feature activation shows the same decline
($\bk=-0.535$, $p=3.0\times10^{-90}$). See Figures~\ref{fig:fig1}
and~\ref{fig:fig4}.

\paragraph{H2 (paired).}
Among the 100 questions correct at $\kof{0}$, the per-question
signal-collapse level is associated with the answer-flip level: Spearman
$\rho=0.731$ ($p=3.6\times10^{-9}$, $n=48$ questions with both events
observed); the feature-signal variant gives $\rho=0.763$. We caution that both
quantities are event-times over the same six $k$ levels, so a global worsening
trend can inflate this correlation in the absence of a per-question link; a
permutation test that preserves the marginal $k$ trend, threshold-sensitivity
sweeps, and bootstrap CIs are required to separate the two and are not yet run
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}). We previously reported a Cox hazard ratio here; we
withdraw it pending a discrete-time hazard model appropriate to the irregular,
six-level $k$ grid. See Figure~\ref{fig:fig2}.

\paragraph{H3 (intervention).}
Mean-ablating the 20 candidate retrieval heads at $\kof{0}$ on the 100
previously-correct questions drops accuracy by $0.82$, versus $0.25$ for a single
matched random-head set. This shows the heads matter for answering at $\kof{0}$;
it does \emph{not} by itself establish that they cause the high-$k$ failure.
Intervening at high $k$ in feature mode (SAE recall-feature re-injection)
recovers a modest $0.161$ (95\% CI $[0.032,0.290]$) of the 31 flipped answers.
Because the features are selected in-sample and only one random control is run,
we read H3 as suggestive rather than dispositive; stronger controls
(many random head sets, layer- and magnitude-matched heads, sham/anti-recall
feature injection) are listed in \S\ref{sec:limitations}. See
Figure~\ref{fig:fig3}.

\section{Limitations and open issues}
\label{sec:limitations}
We state these prominently because several are potential confounds for the
headline claims, not merely scope caveats. They define our planned next round.

\paragraph{In-sample SAE feature selection (label leakage).} Recall features are
chosen by correlation with correctness on the same $(q,k)$ samples on which H1/H2/H3
feature results are then reported. This is circular. The fix is a
discovery/held-out question split with feature and layer selection on discovery
only, plus random-feature, anti-correlated-feature, and magnitude-matched
controls; the feature results should be read as in-sample proxies until then.

\paragraph{Unnormalized attention mass (dilution).} We report absolute
retrieval-head attention mass on the gold span. As $k$ grows the key set grows,
so mass on any fixed span can fall by dilution alone. Required controls: mass
normalized by the gold-token fraction, enrichment over uniform, and
length/position-matched distractor- and random-span baselines.

\paragraph{Sliding-window visibility as a competing mechanism.} Gemma-2
interleaves sliding-window (4{,}096) and global layers. With gold position
randomized and context growing, high-$k$ conditions place gold outside the
sliding window for many layers, so part of the attention decline may be
architectural rather than emergent. We must stratify by in/out-of-window gold,
analyze global layers separately, and add a gold-stays-visible condition.

\paragraph{H2 shared-trend artifact and Cox fragility.} Collapse- and flip-times
live on the same six-level $k$ grid; a global trend can induce correlation
without a per-question link. We need a permutation test preserving the marginal
$k$ trend, threshold sweeps ($\text{collapse\_frac}\in[0.3,0.7]$), bootstrap CIs,
and a report of how often collapse \emph{precedes} flip. The previously reported
Cox HR is withdrawn pending a discrete-time hazard model with only prior/current
$k$ signal.

\paragraph{Causal strength.} H3a shows the heads matter at $\kof{0}$, not that
they cause high-$k$ rot; H3b recovery is modest and rests on in-sample features.
Stronger controls (many random head sets, layer-/magnitude-matched heads, sham
and anti-recall injection, harm checks on already-correct high-$k$ items) are
needed before any causal language.

\paragraph{Heuristic grader.} Substring/token-F1 correctness drives accuracy,
feature selection, flip detection, and recovery detection. We will audit a
stratified sample (correct/wrong/flipped/recovered) against human or LLM-judge
labels, report grader precision/recall, and re-run headline stats on the
H2/H3-critical examples.

\paragraph{Benchmark variant and sampling.} Long gold sessions are trimmed to a
turn window around the \texttt{has\_answer} evidence ($\approx$X\% of questions;
to be reported), making this a controlled LongMemEval-S \emph{variant}; we will
compare trimmed vs.\ untrimmed behavior. Gold position is sampled once per
$(q,k)$; given the large position effect ($\beta=0.446$), multiple position seeds
are needed. Results are single-model (Gemma-2-2B-it) and single-seed; eager
attention is required for the hooks.

\section{Related work}
\paragraph{Behavioral context degradation.} ``Lost in the middle''
\citep{liu2023lost} shows accuracy depends on evidence position within long
contexts; ``context rot'' reports \citep{chroma2024contextrot} document accuracy
decay as inputs lengthen. These are behavioral and largely correlational; our
contribution is a paired, evidence-in-window design that pairs the behavior with
internal signals and intervention.

\paragraph{Retrieval heads and mechanistic interpretability.} Retrieval heads
\citep{wu2024retrievalheads} are attention heads that copy salient context into
generation; induction-head and circuit analyses motivate head-level ablation as a
causal probe. We use copy-score head identification but caution (\S\ref{sec:limitations})
that head necessity at $\kof{0}$ is not sufficiency for high-$k$ failure.

\paragraph{Sparse autoencoders.} Gemma Scope \citep{lieberum2024gemmascope}
provides JumpReLU SAEs across Gemma-2 layers; SAE features have been used to read
and steer model internals. Our use is intervention-based but, as noted, currently
in-sample.

\paragraph{Long-term memory evaluation.} LongMemEval \citep{wu2024longmemeval}
supplies the question/evidence material; we adapt it into controlled haystacks
rather than evaluating end-to-end memory systems.
